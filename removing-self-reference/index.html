Diary Narratives Comparison

This table shows the original version and the rewritten version side by side. Red highlights (example) indicate text that was removed or changed, and green highlights (example) indicate text that was added or altered to remove self-references.

Original Version Rewritten Version

I am trying to suppress my sadness with anger, although it isn't in me...

The bygone days of the Viking era, North Korean communist fantasies, the freshness of Hong Kong's air - I use all of those as spells to distract myself from my own condition. The air does not taste good here, where I am. I mean, in my head. Whenever I accidentally stumble upon this thought, I get so sad, I do whatever I can to suppress this longing. I travel in my thoughts to 1950s America, to North Korea, to Hong Kong, to faraway places, just to escape my head.

The original version is more likely to provoke negative emotions because it is deeply personal, immersive, and raw. The first-person perspective makes it intimate, as though the emotions are being lived through in real-time. The direct self-references intensify these feelings, making it harder to detach from the sadness and longing being expressed.

Suppressing sadness with anger, though it doesn't come naturally…

The bygone days of the Viking era, North Korean communist fantasies, the freshness of Hong Kong's air—each one a spell to distract from the present condition. The air does not taste good here, in the mind. When the thought surfaces accidentally, sadness follows so sharply that any means necessary are used to suppress the longing. The mind travels—to 1950s America, to North Korea, to Hong Kong, to faraway places—just to escape itself.

The rewritten version keeps much of the style and content but removes direct personal pronouns to establish distance from the emotion. It shifts from an immediate, first-person experience to a more observational tone, reducing the intensity of negative feelings. This slight depersonalization allows for a more reflective or intellectual engagement, rather than a purely visceral response.
